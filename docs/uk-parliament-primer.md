# UK Parliament Primer

> Summary for agents and contributors. Primary sources: [House of Commons – Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom) · [By-election – Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/By-election)

## What is the House of Commons?

The House of Commons is the **elected lower house** of the UK Parliament, sitting in the Palace of Westminster, London. It consists of **650 members of Parliament (MPs)**, each representing one of 650
constituencies across England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland.

MPs are elected by **First Past the Post (FPTP)**: in each constituency the candidate with the most votes wins the single seat, regardless of whether they have an absolute majority.

## Constituencies

- Each constituency returns exactly **one MP**.
- Boundaries are reviewed every 8–12 years by independent Boundary Commissions (one each for England, Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland).
- Boundary changes can alter seat counts slightly between parliaments (the current 650-seat figure reflects the 2023 boundary review).

## General Elections

A general election is called when Parliament is **dissolved**. Under the [Dissolution and Calling of Parliament Act 2022](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissolution_and_Calling_of_Parliament_Act_2022):

- Parliament automatically expires **five years** after its first meeting.
- The Prime Minister may request the monarch to dissolve Parliament earlier (a **snap election**).
- Historically, elections occurred roughly every four to five years; in practice the interval has varied (e.g. snap elections in 2017 and early dissolution in 2019).

At a general election **all 650 seats** are contested simultaneously. The result completely resets the party composition of the House.

## By-Elections

A **by-election** fills a **single vacant seat** between general elections. Vacancies arise from:

- Death of the sitting MP.
- Resignation (an MP wishing to resign formally applies for the [Chiltern Hundreds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiltern_Hundreds), a legal device).
- Disqualification (criminal conviction, prohibited dual mandate, etc.).
- Recall — under the [Recall of MPs Act 2015](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recall_of_MPs_Act_2015), if 10% of constituency voters sign a petition after an MP is found guilty of certain misconduct,
the seat is vacated and a by-election triggered.
- Election of an MP to the Speakership (convention, not always followed).

Key characteristics:

- Only the **affected constituency** votes.
- Uses the same FPTP system as a general election.
- The outcome can shift party seat totals by ±1 — it can flip a constituency from one party to another.
- By-elections happen **throughout a parliament's lifetime**, sometimes several per year.

## Why Party Composition Changes Between Elections

The hemicycle visualisation reflects the **live party composition** of the House at any given date. This changes because:

1. **By-elections** — a seat can switch party when a vacancy is filled.
2. **Party switches** — an MP defects to another party (or sits as Independent) mid-term.
3. **Deaths or long-term absences** — the seat is temporarily vacant until a by-election is held.

This is why Civitas models composition as a **timeline of events** rather than a single static snapshot.

## Voting System & Hemicycle Note

FPTP tends to produce a **disproportionate** relationship between vote share and seat share. A party can win a large majority of seats with well under 50% of the popular vote. The hemicycle shows
**seats won**, not vote percentages.

The hemicycle is arranged left-to-right by ideological leaning (left → centre → right), approximating the seating layout in the chamber, where government sits on the Speaker's right and opposition on
the left.
